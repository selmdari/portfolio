A desk-sized pill dispenser built for my grandma — two pills, two schedules, fully automated. The goal was simple: make sure she takes her medication every day without relying on memory, and give me a way to remotely confirm she actually did. It runs on a fixed daily schedule, dispenses each pill at the right time, and logs every pickup to Home Assistant over MQTT. Deployed at her home and running since April 2025. The main engineering challenge was dispensing reliably with zero tolerance for jams or missed doses: a curving sinusoidal magazine channel physically prevents capsule tilt, one closed-loop DC motor serves both magazines through motion sequencing, and an ambient light sensor confirms dispensing and pickup via differential detection.

## Motivation

My grandma takes a daily pill on a fixed schedule. The goal was a dispenser that would dose reliably, alert her when it was time, and give the family remote confirmation that the pill was actually taken — something small enough to sit on her bedside table without being intrusive.

Manual pill boxes require weekly refilling and provide no confirmation. Automated dispensers at the high end target institutional, high-volume environments and are priced accordingly. Nothing existed at the point on that spectrum that made sense for a single elderly user at home.

The constraint that shaped every downstream decision: any failure rate above zero is a system failure. A missed dose for my grandma is not a degraded outcome — it's a health risk.

## Mechanical system

### The capsule stacking problem

The core challenge with cylindrical capsules in a vertical magazine is unconstrained tilt. A capsule that rotates past horizontal jams the feed path.

The first iteration attempted vibration-based orientation: an offset weight on the dispensing motor axis, driven through a rapid back-and-forth routine in a funnel-style hopper. It failed for two reasons:

1. Vibration-based orientation is inherently probabilistic.
2. Gelatin capsule coatings exhibit high friction depending on packing pressure, funnel material, and surface roughness.

The redesign discarded active orientation entirely. The question became: what geometry makes horizontal tilt physically impossible rather than statistically unlikely?

### Magazine geometry

The magazine uses a continuous curving channel — a sinusoidal path that constrains each capsule against the wall, limiting tilt to ~15 degrees. At that angle, a capsule cannot reach vertical; it re-centres horizontally under gravity. The failure mode was eliminated by geometry, not by sensing or correction.

![Magazine channel cross-section with capsule in position, annotated contact points and 15-degree tilt limit](/images/projects/automated-pill-dispenser/magazine-channel-cross-section.jpg)

The 15-degree limit was a design target, not a derived figure. The channel cross-section was tightened until tilt to vertical became geometrically impossible, then validated in CAD before printing. Capacity is 34 pills — one month at one per day plus a four-pill buffer, so the family only needs to refill it once a month during a regular visit. The curving path also collapses the feed path into a smaller vertical footprint, keeping the unit compact for a bedside table.

### Dispensing mechanism

A single rotating cylinder serves both magazines. Rotation direction and move sequence determine which magazine dispenses. The barrel has two slots 180 degrees apart, one per pill.

![Motion diagram showing the dispensing sequence for each pill](/images/projects/automated-pill-dispenser/dispense-sequence.jpg)

### Structural design

Base and magazine are monolithic printed parts requiring no support structures. Print orientation for FDM was a primary design constraint. The lid uses a print-in-place hinge. The front panel is acrylic — dust protection with internal visibility so pill count is readable at a glance, making it easy to check the remaining supply during visits.

## Actuation and control

A geared DC motor drives the dispensing cylinder through a custom spur gear sized for the required torque and speed. Position feedback uses an AS5600 absolute magnetic encoder. Absolute reference within the sub-360-degree working range means no cumulative error and no re-homing after power cycles — important for a device that needs to recover silently after any power interruption without requiring intervention.

The motion profile is trapezoidal with controlled acceleration and deceleration to reduce mechanical noise. Full PID was evaluated and rejected: at low speed with low disturbance loads, open-loop motion profiling with encoder confirmation is sufficient.

On startup the system moves to a known home reference. Dispense cycles are triggered by MQTT commands from Home Assistant on a schedule matched to my grandma's medication routine.

## System states and verification

![System state diagram: Idle, Dispense Triggered, Cylinder Moving, Awaiting Occlusion, Pill Detected, Awaiting Pickup, Confirmed, Error, with transitions annotated with trigger conditions and timeout logic](/images/projects/automated-pill-dispenser/system-state-diagram.svg)

Before each cycle, the sensor samples a baseline ambient light reading. When the dispensed pill drops, it occludes the sensor. The differential confirms the pill left the magazine. A flashing LED signals to my grandma that the pill is ready. When she picks it up, the sensor returns to baseline, the indicator clears, and the pickup event logs to Home Assistant — giving the family a timestamped confirmation remotely.

If no occlusion is detected after a completed dispense cycle, the system logs an error. The failure mode is visible and recorded, and the family can be notified via Home Assistant automation.

## Electronics

The PCB is a general-purpose board designed and manufactured for a previous project. It follows a template used across multiple builds: ESP8266 MCU as a removable module, 9–12V barrel jack input, LM7805 linear regulator, MOSFET-switched output for peripherals above logic voltage, and header breakouts for I2C and GPIO. The switched output here drives the 12V LED indicator.

![PCB with MCU module seated showing MOSFET outputs, header breakouts, and module connector](/images/projects/automated-pill-dispenser/pcb-mcu-seated.jpg)

The board has known weaknesses. Ground management uses a stitched fill with manual vias rather than a dedicated ground plane. The LM7805 is adequate for the MCU's low power draw but thermally inefficient compared to a buck converter. The ESP8266 is now largely deprecated in favour of newer Espressif modules. Unused board area increases unit cost and reduces panel density for fabrication. A future revision would target a smaller footprint, USB-C PD input, buck regulation, and an ESP32 with Zigbee/Thread for native Home Assistant integration.

## Result

![Automated Pill Dispenser](/images/projects/automated-pill-dispenser/thumbnail.jpg)

The system has run autonomously at my grandma's home on a daily schedule since April 2025 with zero missed doses. Pill count at each refill has matched the expected count every cycle, confirming consistent mechanical and sensing performance over sustained use.

**Scope boundary:** Designed and deployed for a single elderly user in a home environment. Sensing logic handles the intended operating conditions reliably. Ambient light variation across environments, alternate capsule geometries, and multi-user operation are not addressed and would require further development.
