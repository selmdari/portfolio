<AtAGlance>

A pantograph is a mechanical copy milling machine; you trace a template with a stylus, and a four-bar linkage scales that motion and drives a cutter. Before CNC, operators traced physical templates by hand. I was designing mine in CAD and 3D printing them, but the hand was still in the loop, and every design change meant a reprint.

**The fix:** replace the hand with a motorized XY gantry that executes a CAM path directly. The gantry couples to the pantograph's stylus arm through a lathe-turned adapter; the linkage does the rest. No physical template, no operator. CAD to cut in one step.

That required restoring a machine with no surviving documentation, designing and building a 3-axis motion system from scratch, and wiring a proper electronics cabinet. End result: ±20 µm positional accuracy and a workflow that goes straight from CAM to part.

</AtAGlance>

## The problem with hand tracing

When I acquired the machine, I replaced the wood / brass templates with 3D printed ones. The workflow was: model the part in CAD, print a scaled template, clamp it to the table, and trace it by hand with the stylus. It worked, but it had serious limitations.

Every design iteration required a reprint. Surface finish depended on how steadily I could drag the stylus. More importantly, the template was already a digital file, the physical copy was a detour. The logical step was to remove the hand entirely: replace the stylus with a motorized gantry that reads a CAM file and traces the path itself.

## Restoration

Before adding a digital brain to the pantograph, it needed a proper rebuild. Despite my best efforts, I couldn't find a maintenance manual for this machine; the original company went out of business in the 90s. Before disassembly I documented everything photographically (every component, bearing orientation, and pin) because reassembly would rely entirely on those records.

The castings were cleaned manually and repainted. Smaller parts went through an ultrasonic bath. Every reassembly decision came from the photos and disassembly notes: bearing orientation, preload, lubrication. The locking pin on the linkage head was snapped; I reground a replacement from steel round stock by hand, with the cam geometry needed to lock the linkage assembly in place.

![Restoration process images](/images/projects/panto-cnc/pre-restoration-images.jpg)

One structural change: the original XY stage consumed Z travel. I removed it and mounted the vise directly to the knee, recovering clearance for taller stock.

## CNC Conversion

### Concept

The XY gantry replaces the hand. The stylus arm that the operator used to trace templates is now coupled to the gantry carriage. The gantry executes the CAM path; the pantograph linkage scales that motion and drives the cutter.

The linkage ratio is set to 2:1; the largest ratio that fits the machine's physical envelope. At 2:1, gantry travel of 460x300 mm maps to a 230x150 mm cutting envelope. The reduction ratio also scales down the force the gantry needs to exert: at 2:1, the gantry sees half the force present at the cutter. The ratio is set in two places together: mechanically, by adjusting the pivot positions on the linkage arms using the engraved scales, and in firmware, by updating steps-per-mm to match. Keeping both in sync means the CAM side always works at 1:1, no scaling in the toolpath. It also means dialing in a finer ratio for detailed work is straightforward: adjust the linkage, update steps-per-mm, done.

Pantograph geometry means the reduction ratio is not perfectly constant across the full range of motion, but that has not been a problem for my current use.

### The stylus adapter

The connection between the gantry carriage and the pantograph's control arm required a lathe-turned stepped shaft. One end is 3/16 in nominal to fit the machine's existing stylus collet; the other is 16 mm for a tight sliding fit into the bearing bore on the gantry carriage. The 16 mm bearing spec was chosen to reduce friction at the stylus-gantry interface. This adapter is the single part that physically connects the digital motion system to the original analog machine.

### XY Gantry

![XY gantry: motor mounts, belt tensioners, carriage](/images/projects/panto-cnc/gantry-assembly.jpg)

Cartesian frame, 2020/2040 extrusion, linear rails on all axes. All structural brackets and motor mounts are custom-designed and 3D printed. Belt drive throughout, lead screws were rejected for being too slow at the feed rates this machine needs and the mechanical advantage of the linkage meant a direct drive belt would be enough.

I settled on LDO 42STH48-2804AH NEMA 17 stepper motors (0.59 N·m holding torque). GT2 20T pulleys give 185 N available belt force on X (dual motor) and 93 N on Y (single). Acceleration was tuned empirically: increased from defaults until steps dropped, then backed off 30%. X settled at 700 mm/s², Y at 500 mm/s². Y's single motor is the binding constraint.

Dual NEMA 17s on X enable software tramming: at homing, each motor runs independently to its own limit switch, squaring the gantry without manual adjustment.

### Z Axis

![Z axis motor assembly](/images/projects/panto-cnc/z-axis.jpg)

To size the Z-axis motor, I measured the handwheel torque required to lift the knee at 1.4 N.m. With a planned reduction of 3:1, I selected a 3 N·m closed-loop NEMA 23 coupled to the handwheel shaft via a custom HTD 3M pulley and sliding belt tensioner (the Safety Factor is quite high at 6.4). The closed-loop driver reports stall and torque overrun to grblHAL, which triggers an emergency stop. On an axis lifting ~40 kg, the high safety factor and alarm feedback seemed like the safe choice.

## Electronics

- Controller: SKR2 (STM32F4), running grblHAL firmware, chosen because it's open source and under active development.
- Spindle: induction motor switched by a rated contactor, relay-driven from SKR2 digital output, normally open; power loss or E-stop drops the spindle
- Power: 24 V DC, 300 W supply. AC and DC physically separated, crossings perpendicular, all runs in cable ducting, all ends crimped, distribution blocks color coded
- Signal wiring: shielded, single-end grounded to prevent ground loops (for steppers and limit switches)
- Cable combs throughout: stepper and end-stop cables physically separated in the harness to reduce EMI
- Protection: main fuse, SKR2 fuse, E-stop cuts everything

**Step/dir signal conditioning:** The SKR2 outputs step and direction signals at 3.3 V. The closed-loop Z driver requires 5 V for stable operation, so I built a common-source N-MOSFET level shifter on veroboard to bring the signals up. A quick build that works cleanly for this use case. It lives inside the electronics enclosure, away from machine vibration.

## Result

The workflow goal is met: CAD -> CAM -> gcode -> part, no physical template, no hand tracing.

Commanded repeatability: ±10 microns on all three axes after backlash compensation, measured by repeated passes over a fixed reference with a dial indicator under no cutting load. Absolute accuracy under cutting is bounded by linkage compliance: 20--25 microns X/Y, ~75 microns Z, at center of travel.

![Repeatability measurement](/images/projects/panto-cnc/repeatability-measurement.jpg)

First output: a 6061-T6 aluminum moving base for a robotic platform, 120×80×6 mm, 1/4 in end mill, face milling, contour milling, circular pocketing, drilling. XY position tolerance ±0.05 mm -- within spec.

![Machined part in progress: contour and pocket operations](/images/projects/panto-cnc/robot-platform.jpg)

This project was my introduction to proper electrical cabinet design: AC/DC separation, signal integrity, EMI. That turned out to be the most satisfying part.
